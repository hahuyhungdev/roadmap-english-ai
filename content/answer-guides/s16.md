### 1. What success pressure do you feel most from family or society?

**Common ideas:** salary, title, stability, marriage/home expectations, comparison with peers, visible progress.
**Useful language:** "visible proof of success", "outside expectation", "well-intentioned pressure", "hard to explain."
**Pattern to apply:** pressure source -> why it feels heavy -> your response.

**Version 1 (Clear B2):**
The strongest pressure is the expectation to show visible progress. Salary, title, and company name are easy for other people to understand. Learning quality or role fit is harder to explain, even when it matters more to me.

**Version 2 (Real situation):**

**Version 3 (2026 context):**
In 2026, the tech market feels less predictable, so family pressure around stability can become stronger. I understand that concern. At the same time, I need to make choices based on real fit and future skill, not only on what sounds safe from outside.

### 2. How do you explain your career choices when others expect faster status growth?

**Common ideas:** explain trade-offs calmly, show plan, avoid defensiveness, focus on long-term quality.
**Useful language:** "I am optimizing for...", "longer path", "not passive", "intentional choice."
**Pattern to apply:** acknowledge concern -> explain logic -> keep ownership.

**Version 1 (Clear B2):**
I explain that I am not ignoring progress; I am choosing the kind of progress that fits my long-term direction. A faster title is not always better if it does not build the skills or stability I need.

**Version 2 (Real situation):**
When family expects faster status growth, I try to avoid arguing. I explain what I am learning, what role I am aiming for, and why the current step is useful. Calm explanation works better than trying to win the conversation.

**Version 3 (2026 context):**
Because careers are changing quickly, I would explain that status signals can be unstable. A role that builds durable skill, communication, and judgment may be more valuable than a move that only looks impressive this year.

**Useful language:** "total cost", "future optionality", "financial stability", "not salary only."
**Pattern to apply:** factor 1 -> factor 2 -> decision rule.

Market uncertainty adds another layer. I may accept a slightly less perfect role if it gives stability, but I still want it to build relevant skills. In 2026, safety is not only having a job; it is also staying adaptable.

### 6. When does status pressure motivate growth, and when does it cause damage?

**Common ideas:** useful when it creates action; harmful when it creates anxiety, image chasing, or wrong decisions.
**Useful language:** "energy versus distortion", "status pressure", "external image", "healthy ambition."
**Pattern to apply:** useful side -> harmful side -> boundary.

**Version 1 (Clear B2):**
Status pressure motivates growth when it pushes me to improve something real. It causes damage when I start chasing image instead of fit. The difference is whether the pressure creates useful action or only anxiety.

**Version 2 (Real situation):**
Seeing someone do well can remind me to work harder or communicate better. That is healthy. But if I choose a role only because it sounds impressive to others, the pressure is controlling me.

**Version 3 (2026 context):**
Online status signals are everywhere now: job updates, income content, public portfolios. I try to use them as information, not as identity. If a signal helps me improve, I keep it. If it makes me panic, I step back.
