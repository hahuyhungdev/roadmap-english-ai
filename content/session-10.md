# Session 10: Defending Decisions

**Level:** B2

**Focus:** Explain why a decision makes sense, what trade-off it accepts, and how the team can move forward without turning disagreement into conflict.

<summary><strong>1) Vocabulary</strong></summary>

### Core Vocabulary - High Reuse

- **decision criteria** /dɪˈsɪʒən kraɪˈtɪriə/ (n) - the standards used to compare options and choose a direction
  - _Common chunks:_ clear decision criteria, agree on criteria, use criteria to compare options
  - _Example 1:_ We agreed on decision criteria before comparing speed, risk, and user impact.
  - _Example 2:_ Decision criteria kept the discussion focused instead of turning it into tool preference.
  - _Example 3:_ Good criteria should include user impact, delivery risk, cost, and maintenance effort.

- **recommendation** /ˌrekəmenˈdeɪʃən/ (n) - the option you suggest after considering context and trade-offs
  - _Common chunks:_ make a recommendation, defend a recommendation, recommendation based on risk
  - _Example 1:_ My recommendation was to release the core flow first and delay the optional filter.
  - _Example 2:_ I made the recommendation after comparing risk, effort, and the release timeline.
  - _Example 3:_ A strong recommendation explains the option, the trade-off, and the next step.

- **trade-off** /ˈtreɪd ɔːf/ (n) - a choice where the team gains one thing but gives up another
  - _Common chunks:_ accept a trade-off, explain the trade-off, speed-quality trade-off
  - _Example 1:_ The trade-off was a smaller release, but lower QA and rollback risk.
  - _Example 2:_ We accepted the trade-off because the smaller release protected the critical path.
  - _Example 3:_ A trade-off is easier to defend when the cost is visible, not hidden.

- **constraint** /kənˈstreɪnt/ (n) - a real limit that affects what the team can do
  - _Common chunks:_ time constraint, QA constraint, work within constraints
  - _Example 1:_ Because of the QA constraint, we focused on the highest-risk payment path.
  - _Example 2:_ The main constraint was not development time; it was how much QA could safely cover.
  - _Example 3:_ I used the constraint to explain why the team should not expand scope late.

- **risk** /rɪsk/ (n) - something that could create a problem later
  - _Common chunks:_ release risk, business risk, reduce risk, make risk visible
  - _Example 1:_ The risk was not the UI code itself, but the untested provider response.
  - _Example 2:_ I made the risk specific by naming the user flow that could break.
  - _Example 3:_ Risk language helped the discussion stay calm instead of becoming personal.

- **scope** /skoʊp/ (n) - what is included and excluded in a release, task, or decision
  - _Common chunks:_ reduce scope, protect scope, out of scope, scope decision
  - _Example 1:_ We reduced scope so the team could protect the main checkout flow.
  - _Example 2:_ Clear scope helped us say yes to the core flow and no to late additions.
  - _Example 3:_ The scope decision was about release safety, not avoiding work.

- **priority** /praɪˈɔːrəti/ (n) - the thing that matters most in a specific situation
  - _Common chunks:_ top priority, competing priorities, prioritize stability
  - _Example 1:_ The priority was not adding more UI options; it was keeping payment reliable.
  - _Example 2:_ The priority was user recovery, so we delayed a lower-value UI setting.
  - _Example 3:_ When priorities are unclear, every decision feels like a conflict.

- **stakeholder** /ˈsteɪkˌhoʊldər/ (n) - a person or team affected by the decision or result
  - _Common chunks:_ stakeholder concern, stakeholder pressure, align with stakeholders
  - _Example 1:_ The PM cared about timeline, while support cared about user confusion after release.
  - _Example 2:_ Stakeholders needed to understand the business risk, not every implementation detail.
  - _Example 3:_ I adjusted the explanation depending on whether I spoke to PM, QA, or leadership.

- **alignment** /əˈlaɪnmənt/ (n) - shared understanding about goals, scope, risk, and next steps
  - _Common chunks:_ get alignment, create alignment, alignment on release scope
  - _Example 1:_ We got alignment on what must ship now and what could move to the next sprint.
  - _Example 2:_ Alignment meant everyone understood what we were optimizing for.
  - _Example 3:_ We got alignment before implementation so the trade-off would not be reopened every day.

- **pushback** /ˈpʊʃˌbæk/ (n) - disagreement or challenge, often when someone sees risk or a weak assumption
  - _Common chunks:_ give pushback, handle pushback, respectful pushback
  - _Example 1:_ My pushback was not against the goal; it was against shipping without one more QA pass.
  - _Example 2:_ Good pushback offers a safer option instead of only saying the request is risky.
  - _Example 3:_ I used pushback when the timeline changed but the quality expectation stayed the same.

- **fallback plan** /ˈfɔːlˌbæk plæn/ (n) - a backup action if the preferred option fails
  - _Common chunks:_ define a fallback plan, fallback option, fallback owner
  - _Example 1:_ We felt more confident after assigning a rollback owner and fallback plan.
  - _Example 2:_ The fallback plan made the decision easier because the team knew how to recover.
  - _Example 3:_ A fallback plan should include the trigger, owner, and communication step.

- **timeline pressure** /ˈtaɪmlaɪn ˈpreʃər/ (n) - pressure caused by a deadline or release date
  - _Common chunks:_ under timeline pressure, handle timeline pressure, timeline pressure from stakeholders
  - _Example 1:_ Under timeline pressure, I try to separate urgency from actual release readiness.
  - _Example 2:_ Timeline pressure made scope control more important, not less important.
  - _Example 3:_ I explained that pressure was real, but it did not remove the need for testing.

- **rework cost** /ˌriːˈwɝːk kɔːst/ (n) - the time and effort needed to fix avoidable problems later
  - _Common chunks:_ reduce rework cost, hidden rework cost, high rework cost
  - _Example 1:_ One more QA day was cheaper than two weeks of rework after a broken release.
  - _Example 2:_ The rework cost would be higher if we rushed the API contract now.
  - _Example 3:_ I used rework cost to explain why a slightly slower decision could save time later.

- **decision owner** /dɪˈsɪʒən ˈoʊnər/ (n) - the person responsible for making or closing a decision
  - _Common chunks:_ assign a decision owner, clear decision owner, final decision owner
  - _Example 1:_ The meeting became clearer once we named the decision owner and next action.
  - _Example 2:_ The decision owner made the final call after hearing engineering and product concerns.
  - _Example 3:_ Without a decision owner, the same debate kept returning in every meeting.

### High-Value Verbs & Chunks

- **clarify the goal** - to make sure everyone is solving the same problem
  - _Example:_ I clarified the goal before arguing about the implementation.

- **compare options** - to look at choices side by side
  - _Example:_ I compared the fast option, the safe option, and the phased option.

- **challenge the assumption** - to question the belief behind a decision
  - _Example:_ I challenged the assumption that a full release was the only way to meet the deadline.

- **push back on** - to disagree politely when something creates risk
  - _Example:_ I pushed back on the timeline because payment failure states were still untested.

- **frame the trade-off** - to explain what the team gains and loses
  - _Example:_ I framed the trade-off as speed now versus support load later.

- **make risk visible** - to explain risk clearly before it becomes urgent
  - _Example:_ I made the rollback risk visible before final release approval.

- **reduce scope** - to remove less important work to protect quality or time
  - _Example:_ We reduced scope and kept the main user journey stable.

- **protect the main flow** - to keep the most important user path reliable
  - _Example:_ We protected the main checkout flow instead of adding another optional setting.

- **align on criteria** - to agree on how the decision will be judged
  - _Example:_ We aligned on criteria: user impact, release risk, and engineering effort.

- **defend the recommendation** - to explain why your suggested option is reasonable
  - _Example:_ I defended the recommendation with incident history and QA evidence.

- **offer a compromise** - to suggest a middle path that handles the main concern
  - _Example:_ I offered a compromise: ship core behavior now and schedule the extra state next sprint.

- **close the decision** - to finish the discussion with owner, choice, and next step
  - _Example:_ We closed the decision by naming the owner, scope, and release condition.

- **follow up in writing** - to document the final choice after the discussion
  - _Example:_ I followed up in writing so PM, QA, and backend had the same understanding.

- **separate urgency from readiness** - to avoid treating pressure as proof that the release is safe
  - _Example:_ I separated urgency from readiness by listing what was done and what still needed verification.

### Speaking Expansion Paths

- **Trade-off** can connect to speed, quality, scope, risk, and follow-up work.
  - _Flow:_ The trade-off was speed versus release quality. We could ship everything on Friday, but the payment failure path was weak. So I suggested reducing scope and keeping the risky behavior for the next sprint.

- **Decision criteria** can connect to alignment, evidence, stakeholder concerns, and final ownership.
  - _Flow:_ Before choosing an option, I would align on criteria. In this case, the criteria were user impact, rollback risk, QA effort, and timeline. That made the discussion less personal and easier to close.

- **Pushback** can connect to respect, evidence, alternatives, and next steps.
  - _Flow:_ I would not just say the plan is risky. I would acknowledge the deadline, show the specific risk, and offer a safer path. That makes the pushback useful instead of just negative.

- **Scope** can connect to priority, release confidence, team capacity, and business value.
  - _Flow:_ Reducing scope does not always mean reducing value. Sometimes it means protecting the main value. If the core flow works reliably, the release can still help users even without every optional feature.

### Secondary Vocabulary - Decision And Release Terms

- **acceptance criteria** - conditions that must be true before the work is accepted
  - _Example:_ We added acceptance criteria for error messages before release.

- **rollback plan** - steps for reversing a release if something goes wrong
  - _Example:_ The rollout felt safer after we wrote a rollback plan.

- **phased rollout** - releasing gradually instead of to everyone at once
  - _Example:_ A phased rollout helped us test behavior with lower risk.

- **release blocker** - an issue serious enough to stop or delay release
  - _Example:_ Unverified payment failure handling became a release blocker.

- **decision note** - a short written summary of options, decision, reason, and owner
  - _Example:_ I wrote a decision note after the meeting to avoid repeated arguments.

- **risk threshold** - the level of risk the team is willing to accept
  - _Example:_ The team had a low risk threshold because the feature touched payment.

### Speaking Frames

- **Agree with the goal, question the path**
  I agree with the release goal, but I would question whether full scope is the safest path this week.

- **Decision criteria**
  I would compare the options using three criteria: user impact, release risk, and QA effort.

- **Trade-off**
  The trade-off is clear: the faster option protects the date, but the phased option protects quality.

- **Evidence-based pushback**
  Based on the last incident and the current QA status, I think the full rollout is still too risky.

- **Scope compromise**
  A practical compromise is to ship the core flow now and move the optional behavior to the next release.

- **Risk without panic**
  This is not a reason to stop everything, but it is a reason to add one more verification step.

- **Stakeholder language**
  For product, I would frame this as release confidence; for QA, I would frame it as test coverage.

- **Decision closure**
  To close this decision, we need one owner, one release condition, and one fallback plan.

### Useful Sentence Patterns

- I agree with the goal, but I am concerned about...
- The key trade-off is...
- I would compare the options by...
- The faster option gives us..., but it also creates...
- A safer compromise would be...
- The risk is manageable if...
- From a product perspective, this means...
- From a QA perspective, we still need...
- I am not blocking the goal; I am suggesting...
- To close the decision, we need...
- My recommendation is based on...
- If we choose this path, we should also...

<summary><strong>4) Dialogues</strong></summary>

### Dialogue 1 - PM Deadline Pressure

**PM:** Can we still ship everything by Friday?

**You:**
We can hit Friday, but I would not recommend shipping full scope. The main risk is not the happy path; it is the payment failure path and rollback complexity.

My suggestion is to ship the core checkout flow and move the optional discount behavior to the next release. That protects the date and keeps the user-facing risk lower.

### Dialogue 2 - QA Concern Before Release

**QA Lead:** I am not comfortable signing this off yet.

**You:**
That makes sense. Instead of treating this as a yes-or-no release, I think we should define the exact acceptance criteria. If validation, payment failure, and loading states pass, the core flow can still ship.

The trade-off is smaller scope, but stronger release confidence. I can update the decision note so PM and backend understand what changed.

### Dialogue 3 - Manager Asking For A Recommendation

**Manager:** What is your recommendation? Should we delay or continue?

**You:**
I would continue with a phased rollout, not a full rollout. The reason is that user impact is high if checkout breaks, but the risk is manageable if we expose it gradually.

So my recommendation is: keep the release date, reduce the risky scope, assign a rollback owner, and check support feedback after the first group of users.

<summary><strong>5) Context Flows</strong></summary>

### Flow 1 - Goal + Criteria + Recommendation

I would start by clarifying the goal: we want to release on time without creating avoidable payment issues. Then I would compare options using decision criteria: user impact, rollback risk, QA effort, and timeline. Based on that, my recommendation would be a smaller release with a phased rollout. That keeps the business goal alive while reducing the most serious risk.

### Flow 2 - Pushback + Evidence + Alternative

If I need to push back, I try to make the reason specific. I would say the concern is not general code quality; it is one untested failure path that affects checkout. Then I would offer an alternative: ship the stable core flow, hold off on the optional behavior, and add one more QA pass for payment errors. That sounds more useful than simply saying the release is risky.

### Flow 3 - Stakeholder Pressure + Trade-off

Under stakeholder pressure, people often focus on the date first. I would bring the discussion back to the trade-off. Shipping everything now protects the timeline, but it may increase support load and rework cost. Shipping a smaller version protects the user journey and gives the team a cleaner follow-up plan.

### Flow 4 - Decision Closure + Follow-up

A meeting is not finished just because people stop talking. To close the decision, I would name the option, owner, release condition, and fallback plan. Then I would follow up in writing so PM, QA, backend, and support have the same version of the decision. That prevents the same argument from coming back two days later.

<summary><strong>6) Reading Text</strong></summary>

### Reading 1 - Defending A Decision Without Sounding Defensive

Defending a decision is not about proving you are the smartest person in the room. A stronger communicator makes the decision easier to judge. They explain the goal, the constraints, the options, and the trade-off in language other teams can use.

For example, instead of saying, "This is technically risky," you can say, "The happy path works, but payment failure handling is still unverified. If that breaks after launch, support load will be high and rollback will be slow." That gives the team a real reason to adjust the plan.

### Reading 2 - Trade-offs Make Disagreement More Practical

Many disagreements become emotional because people argue from different priorities. Product may optimize for deadline, QA for confidence, engineering for maintainability, and support for fewer user complaints. None of those priorities is wrong, but the decision becomes unclear if nobody names the trade-off.

A practical answer might be: "The faster option protects the launch date, but the smaller option protects the core user journey. I recommend the smaller option because checkout failure has higher business impact than delaying one optional behavior." This connects decision, reason, and impact.

### Reading 3 - Pushback Should Include A Next Step

Good pushback should not stop at concern. It should help the team move. If you see risk, explain the specific risk, show why it matters, and suggest a realistic path forward.

For example: "I am not blocking the release. I am suggesting that we keep the date, reduce the risky scope, and add one rollback owner. That gives product a release and gives engineering a safer operating plan." This kind of language is firm, but still collaborative.

### Reading 4 - Useful Patterns Noticed

- I agree with the goal, but...
- The key trade-off is...
- I would compare the options by...
- The risk is manageable if...
- A practical compromise would be...
- I am not blocking the goal; I am suggesting...
- To close the decision, we need...
- My recommendation is based on...

**Reusable discussion idea:** A strong decision is not the option with zero risk. It is the option where the team understands the risk, accepts the trade-off, and knows what to do next.
