# Session 14: Boundaries, Saying No & People-Pleasing

**Level:** B2
**Focus:** Speak about boundaries in a calm, adult way without sounding cold or overly self-help.

<details open>
<summary><strong>1) Vocabulary</strong></summary>

### Core Vocabulary - Boundaries & Expectations

- **boundary** /ˈbaʊndəri/ (n) - a limit that protects your time, energy, or comfort
  - _Function:_ explaining limits without blame
  - _Collocations:_ clear boundary, healthy boundary, set a boundary
  - _Chunks:_ I need to set a boundary around..., That crosses a boundary for me.
  - _Pattern:_ respect + limit + reason
  - _Example 1:_ A boundary can be kind if it is clear and respectful.
  - _Example 2:_ I need a boundary around late-night calls during workdays.
  - _Native upgrade:_ I am not available for that, but I hope you understand.

- **people-pleasing** /ˈpipəl ˌplizɪŋ/ (n) - saying yes too often to avoid disappointing others
  - _Function:_ describing approval-seeking behavior
  - _Collocations:_ people-pleasing habit, people-pleasing pattern, stop people-pleasing
  - _Chunks:_ I said yes from people-pleasing, not real willingness.
  - _Pattern:_ fear of disappointing -> automatic yes -> resentment
  - _Example 1:_ People-pleasing looks kind at first, but it can create quiet resentment.
  - _Example 2:_ I am learning to notice when my yes is not honest.
  - _Native upgrade:_ I agreed because I felt guilty, not because I really wanted to.

- **say no** /seɪ noʊ/ (v phrase) - refuse a request or invitation
  - _Function:_ practicing direct but warm refusal
  - _Collocations:_ say no clearly, say no politely, learn to say no
  - _Chunks:_ I cannot do that this time, I need to say no to...
  - _Pattern:_ clear no + optional warmth + no over-explaining
  - _Example 1:_ Saying no early is kinder than saying yes and becoming resentful.
  - _Example 2:_ A polite no does not need a long defense.
  - _Native upgrade:_ I cannot make it, but I hope it goes well.

- **over-explain** /ˌoʊvər ɪkˈspleɪn/ (v) - give too many reasons because you feel guilty
  - _Function:_ describing anxious communication
  - _Collocations:_ over-explain yourself, stop over-explaining, over-explain a decision
  - _Chunks:_ I tend to over-explain when I feel guilty.
  - _Pattern:_ guilt -> long explanation -> weaker boundary
  - _Example 1:_ Over-explaining can make a simple boundary sound negotiable.
  - _Example 2:_ Sometimes a short honest answer is more respectful.
  - _Native upgrade:_ I do not need to justify every limit.

- **resentment** /rɪˈzentmənt/ (n) - quiet anger from feeling used, ignored, or pressured
  - _Function:_ explaining the hidden cost of weak boundaries
  - _Collocations:_ build resentment, quiet resentment, avoid resentment
  - _Chunks:_ If I keep saying yes, resentment builds.
  - _Pattern:_ repeated yes + ignored need -> resentment
  - _Example 1:_ Boundaries prevent resentment because people know what is actually okay.
  - _Example 2:_ I would rather disappoint someone honestly than agree and resent them later.
  - _Native upgrade:_ I do not want to say yes and then feel bitter about it.

- **guilt** /ɡɪlt/ (n) - the bad feeling that you are doing something wrong
  - _Function:_ discussing emotional pressure
  - _Collocations:_ feel guilty, guilt pressure, guilt-driven decision
  - _Chunks:_ I feel guilty saying no, but...
  - _Pattern:_ guilt is a feeling, not always a signal
  - _Example 1:_ Feeling guilty does not always mean you made the wrong decision.
  - _Example 2:_ Sometimes guilt just means you are changing an old pattern.
  - _Native upgrade:_ The guilt is uncomfortable, but it does not mean I should say yes.

- **reasonable request** /ˈrizənəbəl rɪˈkwest/ (n phrase) - a fair request that still may not fit your limits
  - _Function:_ separating fairness from availability
  - _Collocations:_ reasonable request, perfectly reasonable request, decline a reasonable request
  - _Chunks:_ It is a reasonable request, but I still cannot do it.
  - _Pattern:_ validate request + protect limit
  - _Example 1:_ A request can be reasonable and still not be possible for me.
  - _Example 2:_ I do not need to make the other person wrong to say no.
  - _Native upgrade:_ I get why you are asking, but I cannot take that on.

- **protect your energy** /prəˈtekt jʊr ˈenərdʒi/ (v phrase) - avoid commitments that leave you drained
  - _Function:_ explaining limits in everyday language
  - _Collocations:_ protect my energy, protect social energy, protect mental space
  - _Chunks:_ I am trying to protect my energy this week.
  - _Pattern:_ limited energy -> intentional choice
  - _Example 1:_ Protecting your energy is not selfish if you still treat people with respect.
  - _Example 2:_ I cannot be helpful if I am always drained.
  - _Native upgrade:_ I need to keep some space for myself.

### Speaking Expansion Paths

- **boundary -> clarity -> less resentment:** A clear boundary can feel uncomfortable at first, but it prevents bigger problems later. When people know your limit, they do not have to guess, and you do not have to silently resent them.
- **guilt -> old pattern -> honest no:** I often feel guilty when I say no, but guilt is not always a sign that I am wrong. Sometimes it only means I am breaking an old people-pleasing habit.
- **kindness -> honesty -> trust:** Kindness is not always saying yes. Sometimes the kinder thing is being honest early, before you disappoint someone later.

### Secondary Vocabulary

- **emotional pressure** _(noun phrase)_ - pressure created by guilt, expectation, or fear of disappointing others
- **soft no** _(noun phrase)_ - a gentle but clear refusal
- **availability** _(noun)_ - whether you have time or energy for something
- **personal limit** _(noun phrase)_ - something you cannot or do not want to accept
- **approval-seeking** _(noun)_ - trying too hard to be liked or accepted

### Speaking Frames

- **Validate and decline:** I understand why you are asking, but I cannot take that on.
- **Soft no:** I cannot make it this time, but I hope it goes well.
- **Boundary without attack:** That does not work for me, but I appreciate you asking.
- **Guilt reflection:** I feel guilty, but guilt does not always mean I should say yes.
- **Kindness contrast:** Saying yes is not always kind if the yes is not honest.
- **Expectation reset:** I can help sometimes, but I cannot be the default person for this.

### Useful Sentence Patterns

- I understand why..., but...
- I cannot commit to that right now.
- I need to be honest about...
- It is not personal; I just...
- I can offer..., but I cannot...
- I am trying not to say yes automatically.

<summary><strong>4) Dialogues</strong></summary>

### Dialogue 1 - Friend Asking for Help

**Friend:** Can you help me move this weekend?

**You:** I cannot this time. I have had a heavy week and need to rest.

**Friend:** No worries. Thanks for telling me clearly.

**You:** I hope it goes smoothly though. I can send you a contact for a moving service.

### Dialogue 2 - Family Expectation

**Mom:** You should come to every family dinner. People will ask why you are not there.

**You:** I understand. I still care about the family, but I cannot come every week.

**Mom:** So when can you come?

**You:** Maybe twice a month. I want to be present when I come, not tired and resentful.

### Dialogue 3 - People-Pleasing Pattern

**Coworker:** You always say yes so quickly.

**You:** Honestly, that is not always healthy. Sometimes I agree before I check my own energy.

**Coworker:** Are you trying to change that?

**You:** Yes. I want my yes to be real, not just guilt.

<summary><strong>5) Context Flows</strong></summary>

### Kindness vs Honest Limits

I used to think being kind meant being available whenever people needed me. Now I think kindness also needs honesty. If I say yes when I am already exhausted, I may look helpful in the moment, but resentment builds later.

### The Problem With Over-Explaining

When I feel guilty, I tend to over-explain my no. But too many reasons can make a boundary sound like an invitation to negotiate. A short, warm answer is often clearer and more respectful.

### Reasonable Requests

One thing I had to learn is that a request can be reasonable and still not fit my life. I do not need to make the other person wrong. I can respect the request and still protect my time.

### People-Pleasing and Trust

People-pleasing can damage trust because your yes becomes unclear. Others may think you are fine, while you are quietly frustrated. Real trust needs people to know what you can actually give.

<summary><strong>6) Reading Text</strong></summary>

### Reading 1 - Boundaries Are Information

A boundary is not always a wall. Often, it is information: this is what I can do, this is what I cannot do, and this is how we can keep the relationship respectful.

### Reading 2 - The Hidden Cost of Automatic Yes

An automatic yes can feel generous in the moment. Later, it can become stress, resentment, or quiet distance. The problem is not helping people; the problem is helping from guilt instead of real willingness.

### Reading 3 - Saying No Without a Speech

Many people over-explain because they want to sound kind. But a long explanation can make the other person feel they need to judge whether your reason is good enough. A simple no with warmth is often more mature.

### Reading 4 - Kindness With Shape

Kindness without boundaries can become self-erasure. Boundaries without kindness can become coldness. The adult skill is holding both: care for the other person and honesty about your own limit.

### Useful Patterns Noticed

- I understand why..., but...
- A request can be reasonable and still...
- I would rather... than...
- My yes should be..., not...
- The adult skill is holding both...

### Reusable discussion idea

Healthy boundaries protect relationships because they make care more honest and expectations clearer.
