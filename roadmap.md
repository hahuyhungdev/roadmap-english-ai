# English for IT & Business — Full Curriculum

> **Target:** Interview-ready English for foreign tech companies
> **Format:** 1-on-1 with teacher + self-study
> **Total:** 26 sessions + 2 mock interviews

---

## 🧩 PHASE 1 — TECH & BUSINESS _(Sessions 1–6)_

**Goal:** Explain your technical work clearly, professionally, and with business relevance.

---

### Session 01 — Role & Business Value

Define your role (e.g., Software Engineer, Frontend Developer) in terms of business impact — not just job duties.
Explain how your daily tasks contribute to revenue, cost reduction, or operational efficiency.
Use metrics where possible: _"My feature reduced latency by 30%, which increased user retention."_
Practice answering _"Tell me about your role"_ using business-driven language, not just technical descriptions.

---

### Session 02 — System & Stakeholders

Describe the system you work on (microservices, monolith, data pipeline) at a high level — no deep tech details.
Identify key stakeholders: Product Managers, QA, Sales, Operations.
Explain how you communicate with each group and why they care about your system.
Focus on architecture overview and human interaction, not implementation.

---

### Session 03 — Tech Stack & Trade-offs

List the tools, frameworks, and databases you use — then explain _why_ you chose them.
Go beyond naming tools: discuss the trade-offs behind each decision.
Example: _"We chose PostgreSQL over MongoDB because we needed strong consistency for financial transactions."_
Link every decision to a business requirement: cost, speed, scalability, or time-to-market.

---

### Session 04 — Technical Debt & Change Management

Discuss a time you dealt with technical debt or legacy code.
How did you convince the team or manager to refactor? What trade-offs did you accept?
Focus on change management — not just the coding. Show how you balance short-term delivery with long-term system health.
Practice framing technical decisions in terms a non-technical manager would understand.

---

### Session 05 — AI Tools & the Changing Developer Role

Explain how you use AI tools (Copilot, ChatGPT, code assistants) in your daily workflow.
Give concrete examples: generating unit tests, debugging logs, writing boilerplate.
Also state what you _never_ use AI for: security-critical code, compliance-sensitive business logic.
Keep this session operational. Save ethics and societal risks for Session 13.

---

### Session 06 — Documentation as a Product

Treat documentation as a product that delivers value — not a chore.
Show how good docs reduce support tickets, speed up onboarding, and align stakeholders.
Share how you write for non-technical readers. Include metrics if possible.
Example: _"After updating the runbook, onboarding time dropped from 3 days to 1 day."_

---

## 🔥 PHASE 2 — ANALYTICAL THINKING IN IT _(Sessions 7–10)_

**Goal:** Demonstrate structured thinking, trade-off reasoning, and the ability to communicate under pressure.

---

### Session 07 — Root Cause Analysis & Impact Quantification

Walk through a real bug or incident using a structured diagnostic approach (5 Whys, log analysis, fishbone).
Quantify the business impact in real terms: dollars lost, users affected, hours of downtime.
Example: _"This bug caused $5k per hour in lost transactions for approximately 3 hours."_
Separate calm analysis from real-time pressure — that's Session 08.

---

### Session 08 — Incident Under Pressure

Describe a real-time production outage or critical incident.
How did you manage stress, coordinate the team, and communicate updates under time pressure?
Include the post-mortem process: how you documented the incident, identified the root cause, and prevented recurrence.
Focus on real-time pressure, emotions, and teamwork — the calm analysis happens in Session 07.

---

### Session 09 — Defending Decisions & Influencing Without Authority

Role-play a situation where you must persuade a Product Manager, client, or non-technical boss.
Use business language: cost, risk, timeline, user impact — not technical jargon.
Also cover: how to hold your position when challenged by a senior engineer or architect.
Practice staying confident and open at the same time — not defensive, not a pushover.

---

### Session 10 — Taking Ownership of Failure

This is the external side of failure — how you handle it publicly and professionally.
Describe a mistake that affected the team or customers. How did you own it publicly?
What did you say to your manager, team, or stakeholders? How did you rebuild trust?
Practice the full arc: acknowledge → explain → fix → prevent. No blame-shifting.

---

## 🧠 PHASE 3 — PRACTICAL DISCUSSION: IT + LIFE _(Sessions 11–20)_

**Goal:** Handle advanced discussion topics with balanced reasoning, personal depth, and persuasive clarity. Each session includes a debate prompt.

---

### Session 11 — Burnout, Mental Health & Hidden Pressure

Recognize the signs of burnout: chronic stress, loss of motivation, physical symptoms, emotional detachment.
Discuss the culture of "push through" — when is resilience healthy, and when does it become toxic?
Share recovery strategies: setting boundaries, communicating with your manager, protecting your energy.
Bridge to IT: developer crunch time, on-call culture, the hidden cost of always being available.
**Debate prompt:** _"Is a high-pressure work culture a sign of ambition or a systemic problem?"_

---

### Session 12 — Success, Comparison & Imposter Syndrome

Explore how success is defined — and whether the definition you're chasing is actually yours.
Discuss imposter syndrome: feeling like a fraud despite real evidence of competence.
Address the comparison trap: LinkedIn vs. reality, peer benchmarking, the senior developer anxiety spiral.
**Debate prompt:** _"Does comparing yourself to others motivate you or damage you?"_

---

### Session 13 — Money, Lifestyle & Life Choices

Explore the real questions around money that most people think about but rarely say out loud.
Cover: how much is "enough", save vs. spend in your 20s, lifestyle inflation, cost of living, financial independence.
Discuss peer pressure around lifestyle — the cars, apartments, and travel that peers flex on social media.
Also touch on privilege and starting points: why some people get further faster, and what you can actually control.
**Debate prompt:** _"Should you spend your 20s saving aggressively or living fully — can you do both?"_

---

### Session 14 — Technology, Attention & Modern Habits

Discuss how technology is reshaping attention spans, daily habits, and the ability to focus deeply.
Cover: doomscrolling, notification overload, FOMO, the gap between knowing what's healthy and actually doing it.
Explore the paradox of productivity tools that make us less productive, and remote work's blurred boundaries.
Also discuss habit formation: why discipline is hard, why motivation fades, and what actually works long-term.
**Debate prompt:** _"Is technology making us more productive or just more busy?"_

---

### Session 15 — Relationships, Family & Social Expectations

Explore how relationships shift in your 20s — friendships that fade, new connections that are harder to form.
Discuss: dating vs. career priorities, what you actually want vs. what you're expected to want, friendship after work starts.
Cover family expectations: parental pressure, doing things differently from what's expected, the guilt of prioritizing yourself.
Also touch on misunderstanding in personal relationships — how people grow apart without a single dramatic moment.
**Debate prompt:** _"At what point does meeting family expectations become living someone else's life?"_

---

### Session 16 — Identity, Meaning & Personal Direction

Explore the pressure to "know what you want" — especially when you're still figuring it out.
Discuss: changing interests, feeling stuck despite external success, the gap between what looks good and what feels right.
Cover the meaning question: what makes work feel meaningful, and what happens when it doesn't.
Also touch on personal change — how the person you were 5 years ago is different from who you are now, and that's okay.
**Debate prompt:** _"Is it a problem if you don't have a clear life direction at 25?"_

---

### Session 17 — Career Growth & Self-Directed Learning

Discuss how you measure your own career progress — skills and impact, not just titles and salary.
Describe how you learn new technologies independently: side projects, reading code, open source contributions.
Address the gap between theory and reality: design patterns that fail in production, textbook advice that doesn't scale.
**Debate prompt:** _"Is formal education still relevant for software developers today?"_

---

### Session 18 — Career, Risk & Uncertainty

Discuss the fear behind big career decisions — changing jobs, switching directions, choosing the unknown.
Cover: how to decide when you don't have enough information, managing self-doubt, the psychology of risk.
Also touch on luck vs. effort — how much of career success is within your control, and how to think about that honestly.
Practice answering: _"Tell me about a time you made a difficult decision without all the information you needed."_

---

### Session 19 — Cross-Cultural Communication & Global Team Reality

Discuss how communication styles differ across cultures: high-context vs. low-context, direct vs. indirect.
Explore common friction points: when silence is misread, when bluntness is offensive, when "yes" doesn't mean yes.
Share strategies for working across time zones, async communication, and building trust without face-to-face contact.
**Debate prompt:** _"Should global companies adopt a single communication standard, or adapt to each culture?"_

---

### Session 20 — Morality Under Pressure: What Would You Actually Do?

Move beyond ethical theory. Focus on real scenarios where doing the right thing has a real cost.
Discuss: shipping a product you know has a flaw, staying silent about a decision you disagree with, building something that could harm users.
This is not about abstract principles — it's about what you would actually do under pressure.
**Debate prompt:** _"Is it possible to be both a loyal employee and an ethical one?"_

---

### Session 21 — Individualism vs Community: Who Comes First?

Discuss the tension between personal freedom and collective responsibility — in work and in society.
Cover: the "brilliant jerk" problem, open source as a community contract, when team goals conflict with personal goals.
Bridge to broader society: individualism in Western tech culture vs. collective values in other regions.
**Debate prompt:** _"Should high performers be given special treatment, even if it damages team culture?"_

---

## 🎭 PHASE 4 — BEHAVIORAL & INTERVIEW _(Sessions 22–24)_

**Goal:** Master high-impact behavioral answers for interviews — conflict, motivation, negotiation, and self-presentation.

---

### Session 22 — Conflict with Difficult People

Focus on situations involving a genuinely difficult person — toxic colleague, unreasonable client, passive-aggressive peer.
This is different from professional disagreement (Session 09). Here, the issue is emotional or behavioral.
Explain how you de-escalated, set limits, or escalated to management when necessary.
Show emotional control, not just logical problem-solving.

---

### Session 23 — Motivation, Values & Self-Awareness

Answer classic interview questions with depth: _"What motivates you?"_ and _"What are your values?"_
Go beyond surface answers — include a moment when you discovered your own motivations were different from what you thought.
Example: _"I thought I wanted a promotion. I realized I actually wanted autonomy."_
Show how your values align — or honestly clash — with the company culture you're targeting.

---

### Session 24 — Salary, Negotiation & Professional Feedback

**Part A — Salary & Negotiation:**
Research compensation (levels.fyi, Glassdoor, Blind). Understand timing and negotiation psychology.
Practice a realistic negotiation script. Cover non-salary negotiables: remote work, learning budget, title, start date.

**Part B — Giving, Receiving & Acting on Feedback:**
How do you give constructive professional feedback without damaging a relationship?
How do you receive critical feedback without becoming defensive?
The most important part: how do you _act_ on feedback — track changes, follow up, show visible improvement.

---

## 🎯 PHASE 5 — MOCK INTERVIEW _(Sessions 25–26)_

**Goal:** Simulate real foreign company interviews end-to-end and close remaining gaps.

---

### Session 25 — Mock Interview 1

Full simulated interview — 15 minutes behavioral (STAR method), 15 minutes discussion (Phase 3 topics), 5 minutes Q&A.
Teacher acts as a professional interviewer: asks follow-up questions, pushes back on weak answers.
Record the session for self-review. Focus on: fluency, structure, business language, and confidence under pressure.
No detailed feedback yet — just self-diagnosis after listening back.

---

### Session 26 — Final Mock Interview & Gap Analysis

Second full mock interview incorporating lessons from Mock 1.
Aim for consistent band 7–8 level communication throughout.
After the mock: structured post-mortem — identify top 2 strengths and top 2 remaining gaps.
Finish with a personal improvement roadmap: what to keep practicing, what resources to use, what to do next.

---

_End of Curriculum — 26 Sessions_
