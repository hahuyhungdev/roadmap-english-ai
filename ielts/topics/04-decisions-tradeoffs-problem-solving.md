1. Are you good at making decisions?
2. What kind of problems do you usually solve at work?
3. Do you prefer solving problems alone or with other people?

### Baseline Recording Task

Record a 60-second answer:

```text
Describe how you usually make a difficult decision at work or in life.
```

### Quick Self-check

- **Idea problem:** I could not explain the process.
- **Vocabulary problem:** I repeated problem, choose, good, bad.
- **Grammar problem:** I could not compare options or explain consequences.
- **Pronunciation problem:** trade-off, constraint, evidence, mitigation were unclear.

### 2b. Collocations & Chunks

1. **make a decision** - I make a decision after comparing options.
2. **decision criteria** - Clear decision criteria make debate less personal.
3. **compare options** - I compare options by cost, risk, and impact.
4. **accept a trade-off** - Sometimes teams accept a trade-off to meet a deadline.
5. **speed-quality trade-off** - The release had a speed-quality trade-off.
6. **time constraint** - The time constraint forced us to reduce scope.
7. **work within constraints** - Engineers need to work within constraints.
8. **make risk visible** - I make risk visible before it becomes urgent.
9. **reduce risk** - We reduce risk with testing and rollout plans.
10. **root cause** - A root cause explains why the problem really happened.
11. **test a hypothesis** - We test a hypothesis before jumping to conclusions.
12. **rule out a cause** - We ruled out provider latency.
13. **impact window** - The impact window lasted about 30 minutes.
14. **affected users** - We estimated the number of affected users.
15. **short-term mitigation** - A rollback was the short-term mitigation.
16. **prevention action** - A regression test was the prevention action.
17. **rollback plan** - A rollback plan makes a release safer.
18. **phased rollout** - A phased rollout reduces blast radius.
19. **technical debt** - Technical debt can slow future delivery.
20. **pay down debt** - We pay down debt when it creates repeated problems.
21. **own the failure** - Mature engineers own their part of a failure.
22. **follow-through** - Trust improves when people see follow-through.
23. **release confidence** - Evidence improves release confidence.
24. **safer compromise** - A safer compromise is to ship the core flow first.
25. **business impact** - Technical problems should be explained with business impact.

### 2c. Useful Topic Verbs

1. **decide** - I decide after comparing options. / We decided to reduce scope.
2. **compare** - I compare pros and cons. / We compared risk, cost, and maintainability.
3. **prioritize** - I prioritize the main goal. / We prioritized checkout stability.
4. **reduce** - I reduce risk when possible. / We reduced scope to protect quality.
5. **investigate** - I investigate before blaming anyone. / We investigated logs and traces.
6. **identify** - I identify the main cause. / We identified the root cause.
7. **mitigate** - I mitigate damage first. / We mitigated the issue with a rollback.
8. **prevent** - I prevent repeated mistakes. / We added tests to prevent recurrence.
9. **own** - I own my part. / I owned the release decision and follow-up.
10. **justify** - I justify choices with evidence. / I justified the decision with user impact.

### 2d. Useful Adjectives & Adverbs

1. **practical** - A practical solution fits the constraints.
2. **realistic** - A realistic plan considers team capacity.
3. **risky** - A risky release needs a rollback plan.
4. **manageable** - A risk is manageable if the team can monitor it.
5. **evidence-based** - Evidence-based decisions are easier to defend.
6. **urgent** - Urgent problems need mitigation first.
7. **temporary** - A temporary fix should have a follow-up plan.
8. **preventive** - Preventive actions reduce repeated incidents.
9. **calmly** - Explain problems calmly under pressure.
10. **gradually** - Roll changes out gradually when risk is high.

### 2e. Common Vietnamese Learner Mistakes

```text
Mistake: I do a decision.
Better: I make a decision.
Why: Use "make a decision".
```

```text
Mistake: We need discuss about solution.
Better: We need to discuss the solution.
Why: "Discuss" does not need "about".
```

```text
Mistake: This problem is very serious because it affect to user.
Better: This problem is serious because it affects users.
Why: "Affect" takes a direct object.
```

```text
Mistake: We should find root reason.
Better: We should identify the root cause.
Why: "Root cause" is the natural chunk.
```

```text
Mistake: We rollbacked the feature.
Better: We rolled back the feature.
Why: "Roll back" is irregular as a phrasal verb in past tense.
```

```text
Mistake: We prevent the bug happen again.
Better: We prevent the bug from happening again.
Why: Use "prevent + object + from + verb-ing".
```

```text
Mistake: I am confidence with this plan.
Better: I am confident about this plan. / I have confidence in this plan.
Why: "Confident" is adjective; "confidence" is noun.
```

```text
Mistake: The solution has many risk.
Better: The solution has many risks. / The solution is risky.
Why: "Risk" is countable in this sentence.
```

### 2f. IT/Professional-use Phrases

1. **I would compare the options by user impact, risk, and effort.**
2. **The trade-off is speed now versus quality later.**
3. **This is not a reason to panic, but it is a reason to verify.**
4. **We should reduce scope rather than lower the quality bar.**
5. **The first step is mitigation; the second step is prevention.**
6. **The root cause should be specific enough to prevent recurrence.**
7. **A rollback plan should have a trigger, owner, and communication path.**
8. **I take ownership of my part without blaming the whole problem on myself.**

## Part 3 - Core Idea Bank

### Idea 1: Good Decisions Need Criteria

Simple version:

```text
I compare options before deciding.
```

Better spoken version:

```text
Good decisions are easier when people agree on decision criteria, such as impact, cost, risk, effort, and timeline.
```

IT/professional example:

```text
When choosing a technical approach, I compare maintainability, team familiarity, release risk, and user impact.
```

Useful language:

```text
decision criteria / compare options / user impact
```

### Idea 2: Every Decision Has A Trade-off

Simple version:

```text
No option is perfect.
```

Better spoken version:

```text
Most real decisions involve trade-offs. You may gain speed, but lose control; or reduce risk, but delay a useful feature.
```

IT/professional example:

```text
We shipped the core flow first and moved optional behavior to the next release to protect quality.
```

Useful language:

```text
accept a trade-off / protect quality / optional behavior
```

### Idea 3: Constraints Make Decisions More Realistic

Simple version:

```text
We cannot do everything.
```

Better spoken version:

```text
Constraints are not always negative. They help people choose a realistic solution instead of an ideal solution that the team cannot finish or maintain.
```

IT/professional example:

```text
Because of a tight deadline, we chose a small refactor instead of a full rewrite.
```

Useful language:

```text
work within constraints / realistic solution / full rewrite
```

### Idea 4: Problem Solving Starts With Evidence

Simple version:

```text
I need facts before solving a problem.
```

Better spoken version:

```text
Good problem solving starts with evidence. The first symptom may not be the real cause, so I try to check logs, examples, user reports, and recent changes.
```

IT/professional example:

```text
In an incident, we build a timeline, test hypotheses, and rule out possible causes before naming the root cause.
```

Useful language:

```text
evidence / symptom / root cause
```

### Idea 5: Mitigation Comes Before The Perfect Fix

Simple version:

```text
When something breaks, fix the urgent damage first.
```

Better spoken version:

```text
When a serious problem affects users, the first goal is mitigation. A quick rollback or fallback can reduce damage while the team investigates the deeper cause.
```

IT/professional example:

```text
If checkout fails after a release, rolling back the feature flag may be safer than debugging in production for too long.
```

Useful language:

```text
short-term mitigation / rollback / reduce damage
```

### Idea 6: Prevention Is The Real Follow-up

Simple version:

```text
After a problem, we should stop it from happening again.
```

Better spoken version:

```text
Fixing a problem is not enough if the same issue can return next month. Prevention means changing tests, checklists, ownership, or monitoring.
```

IT/professional example:

```text
After a production incident, prevention might include a regression test, a better alert, and a clearer release checklist.
```

Useful language:

```text
prevention action / regression test / prevent recurrence
```

### Idea 7: Owning Failure Builds Trust

Simple version:

```text
People should admit mistakes.
```

Better spoken version:

```text
Owning failure does not mean blaming yourself for everything. It means naming your part, explaining the impact, helping recovery, and following through on prevention.
```

IT/professional example:

```text
If I approved a release too early, I should explain what I missed and what I changed in the process afterward.
```

Useful language:

```text
take ownership / explain the impact / follow-through
```

### Idea 8: Good Pushback Offers A Safer Path

Simple version:

```text
If I disagree, I should give another option.
```

Better spoken version:

```text
Good pushback is not just saying no. It explains the risk and offers a safer compromise that still respects the original goal.
```

IT/professional example:

```text
I might say: I agree with the release goal, but I suggest shipping the core flow first and moving the risky option to the next sprint.
```

Useful language:

```text
respectful pushback / safer compromise / original goal
```

## Part 4 - Speaking Patterns, Grammar & Expression Tips

### 4a. Sentence Patterns

#### Pattern 1: I would compare the options by...

Function:

```text
Explain decision criteria.
```

Examples:

```text
I would compare the options by cost, risk, and long-term value.
I would compare technical options by maintainability, team familiarity, and release risk.
```

Task:

```text
Use this pattern to describe a difficult decision.
```

#### Pattern 2: The key trade-off is...

Function:

```text
Explain what is gained and lost.
```

Examples:

```text
The key trade-off is speed versus quality.
The key trade-off is more control versus more maintenance work.
```

Task:

```text
Use this pattern to explain one work decision.
```

#### Pattern 3: The first step is..., then...

Function:

```text
Explain problem-solving sequence.
```

Examples:

```text
The first step is to reduce the damage, then investigate the cause.
The first step is to collect evidence, then compare possible explanations.
```

Task:

```text
Describe how to solve a production issue.
```

#### Pattern 4: I agree with the goal, but...

Function:

```text
Give respectful pushback.
```

Examples:

```text
I agree with the goal, but I am concerned about release risk.
I agree with the deadline, but I would reduce scope to protect quality.
```

Task:

```text
Use this pattern to disagree politely.
```

#### Pattern 5: What I learned from that was...

Function:

```text
Reflect on mistakes or problem solving.
```

Examples:

```text
What I learned from that was to verify assumptions earlier.
What I learned from that was to separate mitigation from prevention.
```

Task:

```text
Use this pattern to talk about a mistake.
```

### 4b. Topic Grammar In Use

#### Grammar 1: Comparison

Function:

```text
Compare options and trade-offs.
```

Formula:

```text
X is more/less + adjective + than Y because...
Compared to X, Y...
```

Examples:

```text
A smaller release is safer than a full release when QA time is limited.
Compared to a rewrite, a small refactor is easier to review.
```

IT example:

```text
A phased rollout is less risky than releasing to all users at once.
```

Upgrade:

```text
Basic: This option is better.
Better: This option is safer because it reduces rollback risk and keeps the main flow stable.
```

Drill:

```text
Compare two options using "safer than" or "more realistic than".
```

#### Grammar 2: Cause And Effect

Function:

```text
Explain consequences.
```

Formula:

```text
Because of X, ...
This can lead to...
This helps...
```

Examples:

```text
Because of the time constraint, we reduced scope.
Technical debt can lead to repeated bugs and slower delivery.
```

IT example:

```text
Missing validation can lead to failed checkout attempts.
```

Upgrade:

```text
Basic: The bug was bad.
Better: The bug was serious because it affected checkout and increased support load.
```

Drill:

```text
Use "This can lead to..." to explain one problem.
```

#### Grammar 3: Conditionals For Risk

Function:

```text
Talk about possible outcomes.
```

Formula:

```text
If + present simple, + will/can/may...
If + past simple, + would...
```

Examples:

```text
If we release without testing, we may create more support issues.
If we had more time, I would test the edge cases more deeply.
```

IT example:

```text
If checkout completion drops, we should roll back the feature flag.
```

Upgrade:

```text
Basic: No test, risky.
Better: If we skip testing, the release may be risky because the failure path is still unknown.
```

Drill:

```text
Use "If..." to explain a release risk.
```

#### Grammar 4: Sequence Language

Function:

```text
Explain problem-solving steps.
```

Formula:

```text
First, ...
After that, ...
Once we..., ...
Finally, ...
```

Examples:

```text
First, I define the problem. After that, I collect evidence.
Once we know the root cause, we can choose a prevention action.
```

IT example:

```text
First, we rolled back. After that, we checked logs and built the incident timeline.
```

Upgrade:

```text
Basic: We fixed it.
Better: First, we mitigated the issue with a rollback. After that, we investigated the root cause and added a prevention action.
```

Drill:

```text
Explain your problem-solving process with three sequence markers.
```

#### Grammar 5: Contrast

Function:

```text
Balance speed, quality, risk, and value.
```

Formula:

```text
Although X, ...
X is useful, but...
Having said that, ...
```

Examples:

```text
Although speed is important, quality matters more in critical flows.
A quick fix is useful, but it should have a follow-up plan.
```

IT example:

```text
Although the shortcut helped us launch faster, it created technical debt in the checkout flow.
```

Upgrade:

```text
Basic: Fast is good but risky.
Better: Although a faster release helps the business, it becomes risky if the core flow is not tested.
```

Drill:

```text
Use "Although..." to discuss a trade-off.
```

### 4c. Answer Patterns

Part 1:

```text
Direct answer -> process -> simple example
```

Part 2:

```text

```

```text

```

```text

```

Instead of:


```text

```

```text

```

### 5b. Part 2 - Cue Card

Cue card:

```text
Describe a difficult decision you made or helped make.

You should say:
- what the decision was
- why it was difficult
- what options you considered
- and explain what you learned from it
```

#### 1-minute Planning Bullets

```text
Decision: ship full feature or reduce scope
Context: tight release, payment flow, limited QA time
Options: full release / delay / ship core flow first
Criteria: user impact, QA risk, timeline, rollback plan
Action: ship core flow, move risky option later
Lesson: trade-offs should be explicit
```

#### 2-minute Model Answer

```text
A difficult decision I helped make was whether to ship a full payment-related feature or reduce the scope for the first release. It was difficult because the business wanted to move quickly, but the feature touched a critical user flow, so the release risk was not small.

We had three main options. The first option was to ship everything on the planned date. That protected the timeline, but it created more QA and rollback risk. The second option was to delay the whole release, which was safer technically but not ideal for the business. The third option was a compromise: ship the core flow first and move the optional behavior to the next sprint.

To make the decision less emotional, we compared the options using clear criteria: user impact, QA effort, rollback risk, and timeline. In the end, the compromise made the most sense. It protected the main value of the release while keeping the riskiest part out of the first version.

What I learned from that decision was that reducing scope does not always mean reducing value. Sometimes it means protecting the value that matters most. I also learned that a decision is easier to defend when the trade-off is explicit and the next step is clear.
```

```text
In the end, ...
What I learned was...
```

```text
was a compromise:
ship the core flow first
and move the optional behavior
to the next sprint.

To make the decision less emotional,
we compared the options
```

### 5c. Part 3 - Discussion

#### Question 1: What makes a decision difficult?

Framework:

```text
General opinion -> reason -> example -> conclusion
```

Model answer:

```text
A decision becomes difficult when every option has a real cost. For example, one option may be faster but riskier, while another option may be safer but slower. In that situation, people need clear criteria instead of just arguing from personal preference. In work, I would compare options by user impact, timeline, risk, and long-term cost.
```

Simpler version:

```text
A decision is difficult when no option is perfect and each choice has a cost.
```

Retry prompt:

```text
Use "every option has a cost" and "clear criteria".
```

#### Question 2: Should people take risks when making decisions?

Framework:

```text
Balanced view -> acceptable risk -> bad risk -> example
```

Model answer:

```text
Yes, but the risk should be understood and manageable. Some risk is necessary because people cannot wait for perfect certainty before every decision. However, taking risk blindly is different. In software, a phased rollout is a good example of a manageable risk because the team can monitor results and roll back if something goes wrong.
```

Simpler version:

```text
People can take risks if they understand the risk and have a backup plan.
```

Retry prompt:

```text
Use "manageable risk", "monitor", and "roll back".
```

#### Question 3: Why do people sometimes fail to solve problems?

Framework:

```text
Main reasons -> evidence problem -> pressure problem -> solution
```

Model answer:

```text
People sometimes fail to solve problems because they jump to conclusions too quickly. They may focus on the visible symptom and ignore the deeper root cause. Pressure can make this worse because everyone wants a fast answer. A better approach is to collect evidence, test hypotheses, and separate mitigation from prevention.
```

Simpler version:

```text
People fail when they guess too quickly and do not check the real cause.
```

Retry prompt:

```text
Use "visible symptom", "root cause", and "test hypotheses".
```

#### Question 4: Is failure always bad?

Framework:

```text
Balanced answer -> immediate cost -> learning value -> condition
```

Model answer:

```text
Failure is not pleasant, and it can create real cost, especially if users or teammates are affected. But failure is not always useless. It can reveal weak assumptions, missing checks, or process gaps. The important condition is follow-through. If people only apologize but do not change anything, the failure is wasted. If they add prevention actions, it can improve the system.
```

Simpler version:

```text
Failure is bad in the moment, but it can be useful if people learn from it and change the process.
```

Retry prompt:

```text
Use "process gap", "follow-through", and "prevention action".
```

#### Question 5: How can companies make better decisions?

Framework:

```text
General answer -> process -> communication -> example
```

Model answer:

```text
Companies can make better decisions by making the decision process more explicit. They should define criteria, compare realistic options, name the trade-off, and assign an owner for follow-up. This is especially important under pressure because people may confuse urgency with readiness. For example, before a risky release, the team should agree on rollback triggers and success signals.
```

Simpler version:

```text
Companies need clear criteria, realistic options, and someone responsible for the follow-up.
```

Retry prompt:

```text
Use "explicit process", "trade-off", and "rollback trigger".
```
