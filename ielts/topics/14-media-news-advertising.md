## Part 2 - Vocabulary In Context

### 2a. Word Families

1. **inform / information / informative**
   - Meaning: give useful facts or knowledge.
   - Natural: A good news source should be informative, not just dramatic.
   - IT/pro: Product analytics can inform decisions, but the data needs context.
   - Mistake: Avoid `many informations`; say `a lot of information`.

2. **advertise / advertisement / advertising**
   - Meaning: promote a product, service, or idea.
   - Natural: Online advertising is hard to avoid.
   - IT/pro: Targeted advertising depends on user data and behavior signals.
   - Mistake: Avoid `an advertising`; say `an advertisement` or `advertising`.

3. **recommend / recommendation / recommended**
   - Meaning: suggest something to someone.
   - Natural: Video platforms recommend content based on what I watch.
   - IT/pro: Recommendation systems can improve discovery, but they can also narrow attention.
   - Mistake: Avoid `recommend me a video`; say `recommend a video to me`.

4. **influence / influence / influential**
   - Meaning: affect what people think or do.
   - Natural: Influencers can strongly influence young people's buying choices.
   - IT/pro: Interface design can influence user behavior without users noticing.
   - Mistake: Avoid `affect to people`; say `affect people` or `influence people`.

5. **trust / trust / trustworthy**
   - Meaning: believe that something is reliable.
   - Natural: I don't trust every post I see online.
   - IT/pro: Trustworthy platforms need clear moderation and transparent rules.
   - Mistake: Avoid `trustable`; say `trustworthy` or `reliable`.

6. **verify / verification / verified**
   - Meaning: check if something is true.
   - Natural: I try to verify important news before sharing it.
   - IT/pro: Verification matters when AI-generated content looks convincing.
   - Mistake: Avoid `check true or false`; say `verify whether it's true`.

7. **consume / consumption / consumer**
   - Meaning: use or take in content, information, or products.
   - Natural: People consume a lot of short content every day.
   - IT/pro: Content consumption patterns help platforms tune recommendations.
   - Mistake: Avoid `consume news by eyes`; say `consume news online`.

8. **distract / distraction / distracting**
   - Meaning: take attention away from what matters.
   - Natural: Short videos are entertaining, but they can be distracting.
   - IT/pro: Notification-heavy apps can distract users from deep work.
   - Mistake: Avoid `make me distraction`; say `distract me`.

### 2e. Common Vietnamese Learner Mistakes

```text
Mistake: I read news on internet.
Better: I read news on the internet. / I get news online.
Why: Use "the internet" or the natural chunk "get news online".
```

```text
Mistake: I have many informations.
Better: I have a lot of information.
Why: "Information" is uncountable.
```

```text
Mistake: Advertisement is everywhere.
Better: Advertising is everywhere. / Advertisements are everywhere.
Why: Use "advertising" generally or plural "advertisements".
```

```text
Mistake: Ads affect to me.
Better: Ads affect me. / Ads influence me.
Why: "Affect" does not need "to".
```

```text
Mistake: I trust in this news.
Better: I trust this news source.
Why: "Trust" is usually used without "in" in this sentence.
```

```text
Mistake: I usually see short video.
Better: I usually watch short videos.
Why: Use "watch" for videos and plural for general habits.
```

```text
Mistake: Social media make people distraction.
Better: Social media distracts people. / Social media can be distracting.
Why: Use "distract" as a verb or "distracting" as an adjective.
```

```text
Mistake: I check news true or false.
Better: I check whether the news is true. / I verify the news.
Why: Use "whether" or the verb "verify".
```

## Part 4 - Speaking Patterns, Grammar & Expression Tips

### Pattern 1: Habit With Source

```text
I usually get news from reliable online sources because they're quick and easy to access.
```

Use it for:

```text
I usually get news from + source + because...
```

### Pattern 2: Balanced Media Opinion

```text
Online media is useful, but it can be overwhelming if I don't set limits.
```

Use it for:

```text
X is useful, but it can be... if...
```

### Pattern 3: Influence With Example

```text
Advertising influences people by repeating the same message and making a product feel familiar.
```

Use it for:

```text
X influences people by + V-ing...
```

### Pattern 4: Trust Condition

```text
I trust a news source more when it explains the evidence clearly.
```

Use it for:

```text
I trust + noun + more when...
```

### Grammar Focus 1: Present Simple For Habits

Use it to describe your normal media routine.

```text
I get news online.
I check multiple sources.
I avoid clickbait headlines.
```

Upgrade:

```text
Basic: I read news every day.
Better: I usually get news online, but I check multiple sources when the topic is serious.
```

### Grammar Focus 2: Cause And Effect

Use it to explain influence.

```text
Because ads appear many times, the brand becomes familiar.
Personalized feeds can lead to endless scrolling.
```

Speaking drill:

```text
Use "can lead to" to explain one problem with social media.
```

### Grammar Focus 3: Contrast

Use it to avoid extreme answers.

```text
Although advertising can be useful, it can also encourage impulse buying.
Online news is convenient. Having said that, not every source is trustworthy.
```

Speaking drill:

```text
Use "Although..." to answer: Is advertising useful?
```

### Answer Patterns

Part 1:

```text
Direct answer -> source/habit -> small opinion
```

Part 2:

```text
What content/source it is -> when you use it -> why you use it -> benefit -> downside
```

Part 3:

```text
General opinion -> reason -> example -> risk -> balanced conclusion
```

### Expression Tips

- Say `get news online`, not only `read news`.
- Use `reliable source`, `check multiple sources`, and `verify information` for trust.
- Use `influence buying decisions` for advertising questions.
- Use `personalized recommendations` for algorithm questions.
- Avoid saying media is only good or only bad.

### Repair Strategies

```text
I can't remember the exact source, but...
What I mean is, the platform shows me similar content again and again.
A simple example would be online shopping ads.
Let me put it another way: the problem is not media itself, but how we use it.
```

### Part 2 Cue Card

```text
Describe a website, app, or media source you use to get information.

You should say:
- what it is
- when you use it
- what kind of information you get from it
- and explain why you find it useful
```

### 1-minute Planning Bullets

```text
Source: YouTube or a news app
When: short breaks, after work, when learning something
Information: news, tech explanations, product reviews
Useful: fast, visual, recommendations, but need limits
```

### Model Answer

```text
I'd like to talk about YouTube, because I use it not only for entertainment but also for information and learning.

I usually use it after work or during short breaks. Sometimes I watch tech videos, product reviews, or explanations of current events. The useful thing is that the content is visual, so complicated topics can become easier to understand.

Another reason I find it useful is personalized recommendations. If I watch one good video about a programming tool or a productivity method, the platform often recommends similar content. That saves time because I don't need to search from zero every time.

Having said that, I know it has a downside. Recommendations can keep me watching longer than I planned, and not every video is reliable. So for serious topics, I try to check multiple sources and avoid believing a video just because it sounds confident.

Overall, I think YouTube is a practical source of information if I use it with some control. It helps me learn quickly, but I still need to protect my attention and verify important information.
```

### Part 3 Practice

#### Question 1

```text
Why do people trust information online so easily?
```

Model answer:

```text
I think people often trust online information because it looks immediate and confident. If a post has many likes or shares, it can feel reliable even when the evidence is weak. That's why media literacy is important. People need to slow down and check the source before reacting.
```

#### Question 2

```text
Is advertising good or bad for society?
```

Model answer:

```text
It depends on how it's used. Advertising can help people discover products and support free content, so it's not completely negative. But it becomes a problem when it manipulates emotions, targets vulnerable people, or encourages unnecessary consumption.
```

#### Question 3

```text
Should platforms be responsible for misinformation?
```

Model answer:

```text
Yes, at least partly. Users should think critically, but platforms also design the systems that spread content. If misleading information spreads quickly because the algorithm rewards attention, the platform should have stronger guardrails and clearer moderation.
```

#### Question 4

```text
How has social media changed the way people consume news?
```

Model answer:

```text
Social media has made news faster and more personal. People don't wait for newspapers or TV programs; they see updates in their feeds. The benefit is speed, but the risk is that people may only see opinions similar to their own, so their view becomes narrower.
```
